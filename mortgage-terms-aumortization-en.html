Mortgage Terms and Amortization

From: Financial Consumer Agency of Canada

When navigating the realm of mortgages, crucial decisions revolve around choosing the right mortgage term and amortization period. These choices significantly impact your overall costs, interest rates, and regular payment amounts. Let's delve into the intricacies of mortgage terms and amortization with insights from the Financial Consumer Agency of Canada.

Mortgage Term: Contract in Focus
The mortgage term signifies the duration of your mortgage contract, encompassing the stipulated interest rate and conditions. Terms can span from a few months to several years. Renewal is required at the term's end, and multiple terms are often necessary to fully repay the mortgage.

Mortgage Amortization: Path to Full Repayment

Amortization reflects the timeframe to completely pay off a mortgage based on the interest rate for the current term. For down payments less than 20%, the maximum allowed amortization is 25 years.

Types of Mortgage Terms

Shorter-Term Mortgage:

1. Typically 5 years or less.
2. Allows flexibility in choosing between fixed or variable interest rates.
3. Offers the advantage of lower initial interest rates.

Longer-Term Mortgage:

1. Extends beyond 5 years.
2. May restrict you to a fixed interest rate for a more extended period.
3. Imposes substantial prepayment penalties if selling within the first 5 years.

Convertible Term Mortgage:

1. Some shorter-term mortgages can be extended to a longer term.
2. Involves a change in interest rates, typically aligning with rates for the extended term.

Impact of Mortgage Term on Costs:

Interest:

1. Sets the interest rate for a specific period.
2. Can be fixed or variable.
3. Interest rates often increase with longer terms, influencing monthly payments.

Penalties:

1. Renegotiating or paying off the mortgage before the term's end may incur prepayment penalties.
2. Amount varies based on mortgage type and contract conditions.

Effect of Amortization Period on Costs

Monthly Payments:

1.Longer amortization leads to lower monthly payments.

Total Cost of Interest:

1.Longer amortization results in higher total interest costs.

Considerations for Mortgage Term and Amortization Selection

Renewal Considerations:

1. Future interest rate fluctuations can impact your budget.
2. Carefully weigh options during mortgage renewals.
